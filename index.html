Origins of the NY Philharmonic

Origins of the New York Philharmonic explores the impact of five early conductors during the establishment and development of one of America’s most prominent symphonies. The narrative considers the historic and social circumstances that permeated New York City throughout the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, and how this particular context cultivated the roots of the Philharmonic. NY Philharmonic concert programs both in New York City and the U.S. at large, enabled classical music to reach larger audiences and build a community dedicated to the advancement of orchestral music.


NY Philharmonic History

Founded in 1842, the New York Philharmonic is the oldest symphony to operate in the United States. Since its conception, the Philharmonic has commissioned and debuted works by leading composers, such as Dvořák, Rachmaninoff, Beethoven, and Brahms.

German Influence

The formative years of the NY Philharmonic are defined by the success and tribulations of its early conductors. The Philharmonic’s German influence is attributed to the rise of German immigration throughout the 19th century.

Early Conductors and their Impact

Conductors Carl Bergmann, Theodore Thomas, Leopold Damrosch, Walter Damrosch, and Gustav Mahler contributed to the NY Philharmonic in their own way. Their leadership influenced the Philharmonic’s programs, revenue, and vision.
