Conclusion

Image: Modern Day NY Philharmonic

By working with the financial, social, and historic information compiled by the New York Philharmonic, Origins of the New York Philharmonic seeks to explore early conductor impact on the rise of a prominent American orchestra. The project explores the historic and social circumstances that encased New York City in the nineteenth century, such as German immigration and the rise of new music community. Our data exploration begins with Ureli Corelli Hill, the founder of the New York Philharmonic, and ends with the turn of the 20th century under the leadership of conductor Gustav Mahler. Origins of the New York Philharmonic utilizes annual reports and revenue records to track trends in public popularity and prominence. Data visualizations focus on individual composers— evaluating their influences, interactions, and contributions made to the American orchestra scene. Origins of the New York Philharmonic follows Carl Bergmann— responsible for garnering prestige and public interest, to Leopold Damrosch, a technical luminary and financial degenerate, and how the two impacted the rise and fall of the Philharmonic. We examine Theodore Thomas and his reinvigoration of the orchestra, to Walter Damrosch, the visionary responsible for the Philharmonic’s greatest peak in revenue. The creation of a legitimate venue and an ever-expanding tour schedule lead to an increase in audience— across New York City and the U.S. at large. Parsing through singular concert data allowed for an easy analysis of individual conductor impact— how might’ve personal aesthetic and technical preferences influenced fluctuating audiences? First and foremost, Origins of the New York Philharmonic seeks to examine how personal history, lineage, and individual narratives helped cultivate the roots of the esteemed New York Philharmonic.
